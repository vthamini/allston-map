An investigation into urban land acquisition

Harvard's quiet takeover of Allston

Over six decades, Harvard University assembled more than 200 acres across the Charles River — block by block, parcel by parcel — transforming a working-class neighborhood into the university's next great expansion.

By Thamini Vijeyasingam

Background

A neighborhood shaped by institutional ambition

Allston and Brighton were a part of Cambridge for more than 160 years until the town seceded in 1807. Between 1875 and 1925, the population grew by more than 40,000. Harvard took its first steps into the area during this period, opening Harvard Stadium in 1903 and Harvard Business School in 1927.

Since its first major land purchase in 1989, Harvard has come to own nearly one-third of the land in Allston — from academic buildings to commercial properties and empty lots. Of the nearly 360 acres in Harvard's possession, almost 100 sit undeveloped.

"Institutional expansion in Allston and in Brighton has been a thorn." — Daniel O. "Dan" Mee '81, lifelong Allston resident

1897–1979 · Early holdings
The athletic campus takes shape

Harvard's presence in Allston began with its athletic and educational campus. The Stadium opened in 1903, Harvard Business School in 1927.

For decades, Harvard's footprint was restricted largely to parcels donated or acquired to expand the HBS campus and athletic facilities across the river.

1980–1999 · The anonymous buyer
Harvard buys through a proxy — and nobody notices

In 1989, Harvard quietly began a rapid expansion of its Allston landholdings, acquiring parcels through the Beal Company — a Boston developer — to avoid being charged a premium.

Through this arrangement, Harvard accrued nearly 53 acres in just eight years, spending almost $90 million. It wasn't until 1997 that the anonymous acquisitions came to light.

"It wasn't a bad relationship — they didn't have a relationship, and that reflects a historic failing of many urban universities." — Paul Grogan, Harvard's Office of Government & Community Affairs
2000–2009 · The quiet sweep
32 parcels. A decade. Almost no one noticed.

Under President Lawrence Summers, Harvard set its sights on making Allston the "Silicon Valley of the East" — a biotech campus to rival Kendall Square and the Seaport.

Harvard spent the 2000s aggressively buying residential and commercial properties along Western Avenue, Travis Street, and Seattle Street, often through LLCs.

"If we take full advantage of a physical opportunity across the river in Allston — an opportunity to create a campus several times as large as this whole yard — we will have earned the gratitude of future generations." — President Lawrence Summers, inaugural address, 2001
2010–2014 · Consolidation
A financial crisis — and a demolished neighborhood

After the 2008 financial crisis forced Harvard to pause its expansion, the university continued assembling land more quietly.

The Charlesview Apartments — home to hundreds of Allston families — were demolished after Harvard and Charlesview agreed to a parcel swap, providing $65 million toward new residences further down Western Avenue.

"I remember as a kid, my parents were driving down the area. People had these bed sheets hanging out. They wrote, 'I won't leave my home.'" — Dan Mee '81, recalling the 1961 Barry's Corner redevelopment
2015–2025 · Beacon Yards
The $226 million endgame

In just three years, Harvard spent over $226 million acquiring the former Massachusetts Turnpike rail yards — 68 acres between Western Avenue and the Charles River.

As of 2025, only 14 of the 36-acre Enterprise Research Campus have been developed. Nearly 100 acres of Harvard's total Allston holdings remain untouched.

"Harvard, for its own academic and research purposes, doesn't need all the land that it acquired." — Alex Krieger, Harvard Graduate School of Design

Community impact

A neighborhood shaped — and strained

The changes to Allston have reshaped the make-up of its residents. The neighborhood is historically diverse — with a large immigrant population — and home to a vibrant music and art scene. But over the years, the lack of housing supply and creeping rents pushed many families out.

Boston Public Schools shuttered the Jackson Mann K-8 school in 2024. Mary Lyon Pilot High School will close this June. The neighborhood still lacks a fully functioning community center.

"There were parking lots, housing, and some small businesses up and down Western Avenue. It was a place where you start. A lot of potential for development." — Andrew O'Brien, Harvard Business School Chief Operating Officer

Last October, Harvard sold the seven-acre Brighton Mills Retail Plaza — its first land sale in Massachusetts in a decade. In a statement, spokesperson Amy Kamosa wrote that no new acquisitions have been facilitated in approximately 20 years.

The reckoning

An about-face — and an unfinished promise

A long-promised greenway connecting Rena Park to the Charles River has been in planning for two decades. It is now in its final stages of construction — but critics say it cuts short at an HBS parking lot, falling well short of the original design.

"Unless and until Harvard decides to complete its Greenway promise, that space will seem as if it belongs to the ERC rather than belonging to the broader neighborhood." — Alex Krieger, Harvard Graduate School of Design

Harvard has been able to fill in some gaps — the Ed Portal provides free after-school programming, and new retail along Western Avenue has given the corridor new life. But as the largest sole landowner in Allston, residents have looked to Harvard to play a larger role in making the neighborhood more vibrant and affordable.

"As we look ahead, Harvard is taking a phased, intentional approach to development in this area," spokesperson Amy Kamosa wrote.

A neighborhood in negotiation

Harvard's Allston holdings now encompass the Business School campus, the athletic complex, the Enterprise Research Campus, and the vast undeveloped Beacon Yards site. New retail — Molly's Bookstore, FiDO Pizza, Park Bagelry — has arrived along Western Avenue with Allston Labworks.

Residents, city officials, and the university continue to negotiate through the Article 80 process and the Harvard Allston Task Force what kind of neighborhood Allston will become.

"I get nostalgic. But I get it — you just have to look. Allston looks a lot better today than it did 30, 40 years ago." — Daniel O. "Dan" Mee '81, lifelong Allston resident
